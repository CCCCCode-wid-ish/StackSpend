# Economics

To estimate whether this tool is worth deploying for Credex, I start from the value of a converted lead rather than from generic top-of-funnel traffic. Suppose the average company that books a Credex consultation after using StackSpend ultimately purchases $12,000 in annual credits through Credex. If Credex earns an effective 12% gross margin on that transaction, the gross profit per converted customer is about $1,440 per year.

Not every audit will become a customer, so the real question is what an audit completion is worth in expectation. Assume the funnel looks like this:

- 100 audit completions
- 12 consultation bookings
- 4 qualified opportunities
- 2 credit purchases

At 2 closed purchases per 100 audits and $1,440 gross profit per customer, 100 audits generate about $2,880 gross profit. That means one completed audit is worth roughly $28.80 in expected gross profit before operating costs. Even if those assumptions are cut in half, an audit completion is still worth around $14, which is meaningful for a lightweight self-serve tool.

Now CAC by channel:

- Founder and operator DMs: mostly time cost. If 3 hours of outreach produces 10 completed audits and I value that labor at $25/hour, CAC per audit is about $7.50.
- Organic social teardown posts: if 2 hours of writing and posting produce 15 completed audits, that is about $3.33 CAC per audit on a labor basis.
- Community posts and replies: if 2 hours yields 8 completed audits, CAC is about $6.25 per audit.
- Referral or share loop: effectively near-zero direct CAC once the report pages start circulating.

Against an expected value of even $14 to $28.80 per audit, those channels can work.

The profitability hinge is conversion from audit to booked consultation to credit purchase. At the lower bound, assume:

- Audit completed to consultation booked: 8%
- Consultation booked to credit purchase: 15%

That yields 1.2 paying customers per 100 audits. At $1,440 gross profit per customer, 100 audits produce $1,728 gross profit, or $17.28 per audit. In that scenario, any acquisition channel under roughly $17 CAC per completed audit is still attractive.

What would need to be true for this tool to drive $1M ARR in 18 months? If Credex earns $1,440 gross profit per customer per year, it would need about 695 active annual converted customers to create $1M in gross-profit-style ARR contribution. If the blended funnel converts 2% of completed audits into customers, the product would need about 34,750 audit completions over 18 months, or roughly 1,930 per month on average. That is ambitious but not absurd if the share loop works, content compounds, and Credex has a distribution edge into operator and finance audiences.

The key takeaway is that this does not need massive consumer-style volume. It needs a focused audience, clear savings signals, and a strong handoff into a consultation or credit-purchase workflow. If the tool attracts the right users, the economics can work long before traffic looks large.
