# Go To Market

The exact target user for StackSpend is a founder-led head of engineering or finance-conscious engineering manager at a 10 to 80 person software company that started buying AI tools quickly and now has spend spread across personal subscriptions, a few team plans, and at least one API account. This is not "startups" in the abstract. It is specifically the person who approved Cursor, ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot, or Windsurf spend because the team needed speed, but who now has no clean answer when someone asks, "What are we actually paying every month, and is any of it duplicated?"

The trigger moment is usually not "I want an AI audit" in the abstract. It is something more specific: budgeting season, a new finance hire asking for software rationalization, a founder realizing multiple people are expense-reimbursing overlapping tools, or an engineering lead wondering whether the team really needs team-tier plans for three different vendors. Right before they want this tool, they are likely Googling things like "Cursor teams pricing vs pro," "ChatGPT business vs enterprise," "Claude team pricing," "AI tools for developers cost," or "how to manage AI software spend." On social, they are often scrolling posts about AI coding tool stacks, "my dev team uses 6 copilots," or procurement and budget threads from startup operators.

The channels I would use for the first 100 users are narrow and manual, not broad and vague. First, I would post teardown-style content on X and LinkedIn showing anonymized examples of obviously duplicated AI spend. Second, I would DM people in founder, operator, and engineering communities with a concrete hook: "I built a free audit that tells you whether your AI stack is oversized or duplicated. Want me to run one on your current tools?" Third, I would share directly in specific communities where this pain is credible: Indie Hackers, SaaS operator Slack groups, early-stage founder communities, r/SaaS, r/startups, and small engineering leadership Discords. Fourth, I would use the shareable report pages as the product-led loop: every audit should feel screenshot-worthy enough that someone forwards it internally or posts it publicly.

For the first 30 days with zero paid budget, I would aim for:

- 20 users from direct outreach to founders and engineering managers in my network and second-degree network.
- 30 users from posting 5 to 7 short pricing teardown posts with a CTA to run a free audit.
- 25 users from communities where people already discuss AI coding stacks and software budgets.
- 25 users from shares of report pages and founder-to-founder forwarding.

The unfair distribution channel for Credex is not generic content. It is access to a finance-minded audience that already cares about software purchasing discipline and credits arbitrage. If Credex can pair this product with guidance on how to shift eligible spend into credits or negotiated procurement paths, the audit becomes a top-of-funnel wedge rather than just a calculator.

If this is working in week 1, I would expect numbers that are small but concrete: 40 completed audits, 10 shared report links, 8 inbound conversations from founders or finance owners, and at least 3 users saying some version of, "I did not realize we were paying for overlapping tools." That kind of response would be a better signal than raw traffic because it proves the problem framing is landing with the right buyer.
