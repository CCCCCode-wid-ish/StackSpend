# User Interviews

## Interview 1

**Name:** Hari Prasad
**Role:** Co-founder and CEO
**Company stage:** Early-stage startup
**Company:** Helaroc Pvt Ltd

**Direct quotes:**
- "Cursor, antigravity."
- "Depends on how good the response is and the build quality."
- "Yes."
- "Me."
- "Very rare."
- "I would like to consolidate tools."
- "Depends on how it functions."
- "If it's just another app that just manages spending and gives a report, that will make it useless."

**Most surprising thing they said:**  
The most surprising thing was how directly the decision came back to product quality rather than just price. Even at a startup leadership level, the willingness to pay seemed tied more to response quality and build quality than to a formal budgeting process.

**What it changed about my design:**  
This pushed me to make the audit feel more actionable than a generic reporting dashboard. It reinforced that the product needs to help users consolidate overlapping tools and explain why a recommendation matters, not just show spend totals.

## Interview 2

**Name:** Bhoomika Shinde
**Role:** BBA student
**Company stage:** Student / individual user

**Direct quotes:**
- "Mostly ChatGPT, Canva AI, Grammarly, and sometimes Gemini for assignments or presentations."
- "For students, affordability matters a lot, so we usually only upgrade when free features are limiting."
- "ChatGPT, Gemini, and Grammarly sometimes do similar things for writing and idea generation."
- "Usually monthly. If we stop using it regularly, we cancel it quickly because students don't want unnecessary subscriptions."
- "If it clearly showed where I'm wasting money or which subscriptions I'm not actively using, I'd trust it more."
- "If it gives the same advice to everyone without understanding student budgets or actual usage."

**Most surprising thing they said:**  
The most surprising part was how sensitive student users are to even small recurring subscriptions. Unlike companies, they revisit spending quickly and cancel fast when a tool stops feeling essential.

**What it changed about my design:**  
This made me think more about low-budget users and the importance of honest low-savings outputs. It also pushed me to make the product useful even when the best recommendation is to keep a lightweight setup instead of forcing unnecessary savings claims.

## Interview 3

**Name:** Jeevan R
**Role:** BTech student
**Company stage:** Student / builder / project user

**Direct quotes:**
- "Mainly ChatGPT, GitHub Copilot, Cursor, Claude, and sometimes Perplexity."
- "If it improves coding productivity, debugging speed, or helps during development projects, then it's worth paying for."
- "ChatGPT, Claude, and Cursor overlap a lot for coding help."
- "Copilot and Cursor also solve similar developer workflows."
- "Students usually keep only tools they actively use."
- "If another AI tool performs better for coding at a lower cost, or if free models become good enough, we'd switch."
- "If it showed actual usage statistics, overlap between tools, and suggested cheaper alternatives based on workflows."
- "If it only gives general cost-cutting advice without understanding developer workflows or coding use cases."

**Most surprising thing they said:**  
The most surprising thing was how normal it has become for technically strong students to pay for multiple overlapping coding tools at the same time, especially during internship prep or project-heavy periods.

**What it changed about my design:**  
This strengthened the case for per-tool analysis in coding workflows. It also made me prioritize recommendations that compare similar developer tools across the same stack instead of treating each subscription as an isolated purchase.
